## Task 1: 5 Interesting Concepts Learned from the Lectures

### 1. Tricking the Human Eye
Our eyes only have three color receptors (Red, Green, and Blue). Because our brains blend these signals, screens do not need to output a true light spectrum. They just mix RGB pixels to trick us into seeing millions of colors.

### 2. Prioritising Brightness over Color
Humans notice changes in brightness much more than changes in color. Video systems use color spaces like YCbCr to separate brightness from color. They compress data by throwing away a lot of color detail while keeping the brightness sharp, and our eyes barely notice.

### 3. Screen Math vs. Code
Mathematically, an image is a continuous field of light. Digitally, it is a grid of pixels. In code, the origin $(0,0)$ sits at the top-left corner, and pixels are indexed by row first, then column `[y, x]`.

### 4. The Danger of Shrinking Images
If you shrink an image by just deleting rows and columns of pixels, you get jagged edges and weird patterns (aliasing). To downsample correctly, you must blur the image first to wipe out fine details that are too small for the new, smaller grid to handle.

### 5. Guessing Pixels when Zooming In
To make an image bigger, a computer must invent new pixels using one of three math methods:
* **Nearest Neighbor**: Copies the closest pixel. Fast, but looks blocky.
* **Bilinear**: Averages the 4 nearest pixels. Smoother, but a bit blurry.
* **Bicubic**: Averages 16 surrounding pixels. Slowest, but creates the sharpest, highest-quality look.

## Task 2: Research on Healthcare Reports & Medical Imaging Reports

### Types of Healthcare Reports
Healthcare systems rely on several distinct document types to track, communicate, and preserve patient medical data. The primary categories include:
* **Diagnostic Reports**: Written summaries generated by specialists (pathologists, radiologists) interpreting lab tests, tissue biopsies, or imaging scans.
* **Clinical/Progress Notes**: Ongoing documentation written by physicians or nurses detailing a patient's daily status, symptoms, and response to treatment during hospital stays or outpatient visits.
* **Discharge Summaries**: Comprehensive overviews provided when a patient leaves a facility, outlining the reason for admission, significant findings, treatments administered, medications prescribed, and follow-up plans.
* **Operative Reports**: Detailed, step-by-step narratives written by a surgeon immediately following a surgical procedure, documenting the findings, techniques used, and any complications.

### Focus: Medical Imaging Reports
A Medical Imaging Report is a specialized diagnostic document authored by a radiologist. It translates complex visual data captured by imaging modalities into structured, actionable medical text for the referring physician.

#### 1. Common Modalities Involved
* **X-Ray**: Utilizes ionizing radiation; ideal for evaluating bone fractures, joint dislocations, and acute chest infections.
* **Computed Tomography (CT)**: Takes cross-sectional X-ray "slices" of the body, which are digitally reconstructed into 3D volumes. Exceptional for evaluating internal trauma, vascular structures, and tumors.
* **Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)**: Uses strong magnetic fields and radio waves to map hydrogen atoms in the body. It provides unparalleled soft-tissue contrast, vital for neurology (brain/spine) and musculoskeletal imaging.
* **Ultrasound**: Uses high-frequency sound waves to generate real-time moving images. Safe (non-ionizing) and heavily used for obstetrics, cardiac imaging (echocardiograms), and fluid assessments.

#### 2. Key Components of an Imaging Report
A standard, professional medical imaging report strictly follows a structured format. The following report Section should have the description in that particular manner:

* **Patient & Exam Info**
  * This section lists basic demographic data and the exam date.
  * It contains a unique identification number for patient safety.
  * The exact modality and specific protocol are explicitly detailed.
  * An example entry includes "CT Abdomen with IV Contrast".

* **Clinical History**
  * This features a brief statement written by your referring physician.
  * It outlines your current symptoms or specific medical questions.
  * The text guides the radiologist on what to look for.
  * An example entry includes "Rule out appendicitis".

* **Comparison**
  * This section references your previous imaging studies if available.
  * It focuses strictly on the same anatomical body area.
  * The radiologist evaluates changes in your condition over time.
  * It notes whether findings have improved, worsened, or stayed stable.

* **Technique**
  * This provides a technical summary of the scan's execution.
  * It documents the specific views and image angles taken.
  * The text lists any contrast agents administered to you.
  * It notes specific patient positioning used during the scan.

* **Findings**
  * This section serves as the core narrative of the report.
  * The radiologist systemically describes all visualized organs and structures.
  * It details normal characteristics to confirm healthy tissue.
  * It notes abnormal pathologies, spots, or injuries discovered.

* **Impression**
  * This is the most critical section of the entire report.
  * It provides a concise summary synthesized directly from the findings.
  * The radiologist offers a definitive diagnosis whenever possible.
  * It lists a differential diagnosis or next-step testing recommendations.
